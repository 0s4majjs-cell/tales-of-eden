# Beyond the Walls

The walls weren’t just walls. They were horizons made of steel—towering plates welded together into a sheer cliff that circled the Berlin Safe Zone, cold and unbreakable. They said the walls were built to keep the horrors of the old world out. Personally, I always suspected they were just as much about keeping us in. I’d lived like livestock at their base for as long as I could remember. The only real escape I’d ever had was through pages: the brittle, recycled paper of old books and half-intact data-slates. Stories became my sky and my sea and my breath. While the air inside the safe zone was filtered and stale, the air in those stories felt endless. They spoke of the world beyond the steel—of coasts made of warm, shifting sand and oceans so wide they swallowed the horizon. Of rain that wasn’t acid and skies that weren’t hidden behind smog and defense grids. A world vast enough that I could disappear inside it and never come back. I wanted to see it. I wanted to walk beyond those walls and become something more than a numbered body kept alive by rations and regulations.
I’d always loved the idea of exploration—adventure, discovery, the unknown. But in this day and age, the unknown isn’t a blank space on a map. It’s a graveyard. The world outside is crueler than the stories ever suggested. Mutant wildlife, scavenger gangs, leftover war machines that never got the shutdown order—every path feels like it ends the same way: with you dying badly and being forgotten quickly. Stories used to be about wonder. Now they are medicine—weak, fragile medicine.

Pills made of ink and lies. They let us taste freedom we’ll never have, pretend we’re somewhere else for a few hours, and when they’re honest, they remind us of everything terrible we’ve done to this planet. The wars, the fallout, the experiments, the walls.
I don’t like this world. Not at all. That’s what makes stories so painfully beautiful. They let you escape, but only halfway. Your mind runs a thousand miles, but your body never leaves the room. You’re still here, caged in steel, chained to a life you never chose. In the end, there’s only one absolute escape: death. But that terrifies me too. *I don’t want to die.*

***I don’t want to die.***

I woke up with that sentence echoing in my head, as if someone had stood beside my bed and whispered it in my ear. My hand crawled up my face, fingers dragging across my eyes, rubbing at the dull ache behind my lids before sliding down my cheeks. I yawned, a hollow, exhausted sound that felt bigger than the room. For a long moment I just sat there and stared at the wall. Cracked plaster. A rust stain bleeding from a pipe. Nothing dramatic—just the kind of ugliness you stop seeing after a while. “I don’t want to die,” drifted up again from wherever my dreams had thrown it. I didn’t bother chasing the memory. Dreams fade on their own. They always do. Or maybe we just stop wanting to remember them.

 

I told myself I should get up. Right now. Do something with the day before it did something to me. Instead my body sank back into the mattress, which creaked its usual protest. Ten more minutes wouldn’t hurt. Just ten. Then I’d definitely get up.
My eyes followed the familiar lines in the ceiling, the branching cracks I’d already traced a hundred times. My head felt hollow somehow—empty but heavy, like someone had scooped out all the useful parts and replaced them with wet sand. I wanted to escape. The thought came simple and clean: *Why don’t I just escape?* It was such an obvious question that it surprised me. Yeah. Why don’t I?

Something snapped awake in me—impulse, panic, maybe some dumb spark of courage—and I shot upright so fast my vision blurred. I rubbed my eyes too hard, like I could scrub the hesitation out of them, and by the time the world came back into focus, my hands were already moving. Clothes. Rations. A small water canister. My books. Folded papers. My favorite pen. By the time I actually realized what I was doing, my bag was already packed and sitting on the bed, staring back at me like evidence.

And then the spark was gone. My shoulders sagged. My heart slowed. All that sudden energy, that rush of **yes, this is it**—gone in seconds.
“What the hell was I thinking?” I muttered. The sarcastic part of my brain chimed in immediately: *Oh, right. Great plan. Walk out of the Berlin Safe Zone into a wasteland full of mutants and desperate people with guns, and see what happens. Maybe write a poetic death scene on the way down. Brilliant.*

 

Still, the bag sat there, packed and waiting, like a dare I’d given myself. I stared at it until the details blurred—the frayed strap, the patched canvas, the ink stain near the zipper where my pen had exploded months back. Eventually I stood, slung it over my shoulder, and opened the door.
The corridor outside was a narrow concrete throat lit by tired strips of LED. Steel doors lined both sides, each stamped with a number and a lock that had never really been tested. A few neighbors lurked in their doorways, talking in low voices or just leaning against the wall. Eyes flicked toward the bag on my shoulder, then away. No one asked where I was going. No one told me not to.

When I stepped out of the building, the air bit harder than it had inside. The outer block huddled at the base of the wall like a cluster of metal boxes, prefab panels patched with salvaged steel and plastic. Cables sagged between lampposts. Somewhere far off, a generator coughed and steadied. Beyond the roofs and fences, the wall itself towered—an unbroken curtain of steel plates, welding scars and rust streaks catching the last smear of daylight.

The sun was already sinking, pale and weak behind the haze. If I wanted any chance, I’d have to wait for full dark. During the day, the scanners and patrols were precise. At night they were still there, just… human. More tired, more bored, more likely to miss one idiot running in the wrong direction.



I killed time wandering the alleys, hands in my pockets, hood up. Loudspeakers crackled to life and spat out the usual announcements—ration schedules, curfew reminders, a clipped warning about unauthorized access to the wall. Lights flicked on in narrow windows, squares of yellow fighting uselessly against the coming dark.
When the curfew siren finally wailed and the last smudge of daylight vanished, I adjusted the strap of my bag, pulled my hood lower, and headed for the wall.

The air near its base always felt different. Colder. Thinner. A constant electric hum thrummed through the steel under my feet, the buried shield systems whispering under the concrete. Floodlights perched along the top threw down harsh cones of white, carving the ground into slices of glare and shadow. I moved in the gaps, heart hammering, trying to look like someone who definitely wasn’t about to do something terminally stupid.

Then I saw it: the elevator. A blocky metal box set into the inner face of the wall, its doors shut, the call panel glowing faintly green. Hope flared—for exactly half a second.

Because then I saw them.

Eight 74’s.

 

They stood in a lazy arc around the elevator, armor plates matte and worn, helmets sealed, green visors reflecting the floodlights in flat, disinterested flashes. Rifles rested in their arms, not quite relaxed and not quite ready. Authorized lethal response units—*protectors*, on paper. Prison guards, in practice.

The smart move was obvious. Turn around. Go home. Pretend this had never gone further than the inside of my head. I even lifted my foot, ready to pivot back toward the safety of my room and my books and my quiet, pointless life.

Instead, I moved forward.

One step, then another, the crunch of gravel under my boots far too loud in my ears. My walk became a fast walk, then a jog, then a full sprint straight toward the elevator. Fear screamed inside my skull—**stop, stop, STOP**—but my legs ignored it.

 

The distance between me and the 74’s collapsed. One of them turned, visor angling in my direction. Another’s hands shifted on his rifle. I was absolutely certain that in the next breath, everything would end in gunfire and blood on the concrete.

But the breath came and went.

I slipped past the first pair before they fully reacted. A hand swiped at my coat and missed. Someone barked a command, the words shredded by the rush of blood in my ears. Then I was past the next pair, momentum hauling me forward faster than my fear could keep up.

A laugh tore out of me—short, wild, disbelieving. I was actually getting away with it.

 

I slammed into the elevator’s control panel, my palm smacking the **up** button hard enough to make it rattle. Nothing happened. For two agonizing seconds I stood there fully exposed, feeling eight visors lock onto my back. Then I stabbed another button.

The elevator lurched as if something huge had kicked it from below. Gears screamed to life behind the walls, and the floor began to rise. My knees almost buckled with relief. I pressed my back against the cold metal, chest heaving, listening for the hammer of gunfire that never came.

So far, so not dead.

The ride up felt much longer than it probably was. Through the small grated window in the door, I watched steel ribs and welded plates slide past, all streaked with rust and old repairs. The deeper hum of the wall vibrated through the floor. Wind hissed somewhere outside the shaft, sneaking through gaps in the structure. My ears popped. My stomach did an uneasy roll.

I tried to slow my breathing. My heart refused the suggestion.

 

Eventually the elevator shuddered to a stop. The doors rattled open on a blast of air crisp enough to sting my face. I stepped out onto the top of the wall and froze.

A narrow strip of steel stretched out in both directions, lined with railings, antenna masts, and the skeletal frames of old auto-turrets. Floodlights cast hard islands of white, leaving long shadows between them. The wind up here was sharper, cleaner, and mercilessly cold; it cut straight through my coat, scraping at my skin.

On one side, the Berlin Safe Zone sagged beneath me in a patchwork of dull lights and smoke—tiny, contained, artificial. On the other, the outside world sprawled into darkness: broken towers jutting like teeth, collapsed bridges frozen mid-fall, a pale dusting of snow softening the jagged edges. Above it all, the sky yawned open, raw and vast in a way I’d only ever seen in pictures.

Only a handful of people were ever meant to stand up here—and all of them had clearances I definitely did not. I had a bag, a pen, and a very questionable plan.

One very obvious problem stared back at me from the edge: I had no idea how to get down.

I kept low and moved along the walkway, hugging whatever shadows I could find. My boots rang softly on the steel. Every few minutes, a pair of 74’s trudged by on patrol, visors sweeping methodically. Each time, I glued myself behind a support strut or a bulky turret housing and held my breath until they passed.

 

After what felt like a dozen near heart attacks, I saw it: a narrow metal door set into the far side of the wall, marked **MAINTENANCE ONLY** in flaking yellow paint. It was exactly the kind of door I wasn’t supposed to touch. Which made it perfect.

The handle resisted, but the lock looked as tired as everything else. One solid kick with every ounce of adrenaline I had left snapped the latch. The door banged inward, clanging against the inside wall. I slipped through and pulled it shut behind me.

On the other side waited a spiral staircase bolted into the inside of a steel cage—a cylindrical shaft of bars and steps coiling down the outer face of the wall. It fell away into darkness, the bottom so far down it might as well have been the ground’s memory.

“Fantastic,” I muttered. “A death spiral.”

Wind hissed up through the gaps, colder than before. I wrapped my fingers around the railing, took a breath that was absolutely not shaky, and stepped onto the first stair. It creaked but held.

Around and around I went. The safe zone dropped out of sight behind the bars. The ruined city beyond the wall rose higher in my view. Rust flaked under my boots. My hand went numb on the railing halfway down. By the time my feet finally hit solid ground again, my legs felt like they’d been replaced by wet rope.

 

The staircase opened into a broad, low chamber carved into the wall’s base—a garage big enough to swallow half the outer block. The air was thick with the smell of fuel and exhaust. Yellow lamps buzzed overhead, casting everything in a dirty gold light.

Rows of massive transport trucks sat in loose formation, engines idling, exhaust fumes curling toward vents in the ceiling. The far end of the room opened into a reinforced tunnel that led into the night. Workers in stained coveralls moved between the vehicles, shouting over the rumble. A handful of 74’s lingered near the exit, scanning manifests and waving trucks through.

If I’d imagined a simple door I could slip out of, this was the opposite of that. The only way out was on wheels.

So I picked a truck.

 

Keeping to the shadows, I wove between crates and maintenance consoles, waiting for the noise to spike before I moved. My eyes scanned for any weakness—an unlocked door, a loose tarp, anything. Most trucks were sealed tight. Then I spotted one trundling past with its rear cover only half-secured, the tarp flapping in the draft.

That one.

I timed my run, darting out from behind a stack of crates as the truck rolled by. The concrete slid under my boots as I sprinted after it, my bag bouncing against my back. I grabbed the tailgate and jumped.

My hands caught the edge of the metal by instinct more than skill. The truck picked up speed, dragging me along. The ground flashed past under my dangling feet—gray concrete, black rubber streaks, the occasional puddle of oil. My shoulders screamed under the strain.

 

“Come on…” I hissed, hauling myself up with everything I had left. My right shoulder twinged; my grip slipped for a terrifying second before I found purchase again. Foot. Knee. Hip. Inch by inch, I dragged my weight into the back of the truck and rolled inside.

The interior was almost empty—just a few strapped-down crates and the cold metal bed. I yanked the loose edge of the tarp down and hooked it into place as best I could. The hangar lights blinked out. Darkness swallowed me, broken only by thin lines of gray where the fabric didn’t quite meet the frame.

For a long moment, I lay there on my back, breathing hard, ears full of engine noise. Then adrenaline gave way to shaky laughter. “You’re insane,” I told myself. “Completely insane.”

The truck rumbled forward, joined a slow procession, and rolled toward the tunnel. Voices echoed faintly—the clipped tones of guards, the tired responses of drivers. We stopped. The hum of scanners crawled over the truck’s frame. Light flared briefly through the tarp seams. I froze, every muscle locked.

No one checked the back.

The engine revved again. We moved on.

 

The sound changed as we left the hangar: the echoing growl of the engine turned hollow, bouncing off the walls of the tunnel. Something deep beneath us hummed—a low, resonant vibration that made my teeth buzz. The outer shield, maybe. Last barrier between “inside” and the world.

Then we were out.

The air that knifed through the tiny gaps in the tarp was colder, brighter somehow, carrying the sharp, clean bite of real winter.

I edged toward the back and lifted a corner of the tarp.

 

The world beyond the wall stretched out under a heavy sky. Ruins unfurled in every direction: half-collapsed skyscrapers, skeletal cranes, broken rail lines, all of it dusted white. The moon hung above it like an old scar. Streetlights that no longer worked leaned drunkenly over snow-choked roads. No people. No drones. No speakers. Just silence and the distant roar of the engine.

The Berlin Safe Zone was already shrinking behind us, a dim glow on the horizon. Ahead was everything I’d ever read about and nothing I’d ever really understood.

I stayed hidden as the truck pressed further into the dead city. Time stretched. The constant vibration of the floor numbed my legs. At some point I realized I had no idea how long I’d been lying there. An hour, maybe more. Long enough that staying on started to feel more dangerous than getting off. I couldn’t risk being carried into some patrolled outpost or mutant nest.

At some point, I’d have to jump.

 

When I checked again, the truck was winding through streets flanked by broken office blocks and crumbling apartment towers. Snow coated the tops of rusted cars and filled in the gaps in the asphalt. The only light came from the truck’s headlights and the cold glow of the moon. No other vehicles. No movement. Just ruins and shadows.

“This is it,” I whispered, fingers tightening on the tailgate. If I waited any longer, I’d lose my nerve. Or my chance.

I tried to time my jump between bumps, watching the rhythm of the truck’s sway. “Two…”

 

The truck hit a pothole the size of my regrets on “two.”

The world jumped. My hands slipped. For a split second, I felt weightless, hanging in cold air. Then the ground slammed into my right side with all the subtlety of an explosion. Pain detonated in my arm and shoulder.

I tumbled across the cracked road, rolling through slush and debris. My coat shredded under me. Skin went with it. The world became a blur of gray and white and red.

When I finally stopped moving, I just lay there, staring up at the sky. The breath had been punched clean out of me. My lungs clawed at empty air. Tiny flakes of snow drifted down into my field of vision, melting on my eyelashes. For a long moment, there was nothing but the roar of my own pulse and the distant growl of the truck’s engine.

Eventually I turned my head. The truck’s tail lights shrank between two ruined buildings, then vanished entirely. They hadn’t slowed. No reason to. Why would a driver check if an unregistered idiot had flung himself out of their cargo bed?

 

A rough noise scraped out of my throat—half groan, half laugh. “God… damn… it…”

Every movement hurt. My right arm lit up with white-hot agony when I tried to push myself up. My clothes were torn open along my side, soaked with dirty snow and blood. My fingers burned where skin had been peeled away.

But I was breathing. I was outside the walls. Technically, this was a success.

I forced myself onto my knees, then my feet, cradling my right arm against my chest. The world tilted for a moment before leveling out. Ruined buildings loomed on either side of the road—empty windows like dead eyes. Snow clung to their broken edges. The air was so cold it felt sharp in my lungs.

I had no idea where I was. No shelter in sight. No plan beyond *don’t die*.

 

So I walked.

My boots crunched through the thin crust of snow. Each step sent a dull throb through my battered legs. The ruins around me blurred together into a repeating pattern of wreckage—collapsed storefronts, overturned cars half-buried in white, twisted rebar jutting out of concrete like exposed bone.

I had always imagined the outside world as loud in its own way. Monstrous roars, distant gunfire, the constant buzz of danger. Instead, it was quiet. Not peaceful. Just… empty. The kind of silence that felt like the world holding its breath.

The cold dug deeper with every minute. It seeped through the holes in my clothes, sank into my skin, then into my bones. My right arm went from screaming to a steady, sick throb. My fingers tingled. My jaw chattered.

“How could anyone want this?” I muttered, voice rasping. “How could I?”

 

I didn’t know how long I walked. Time blurred into an endless series of steps. The sky shifted gradually from dark to a dull, cloud-smeared gray as some version of morning crawled over the horizon. My shadow appeared faintly at my side, stretched and shaky.

I told myself I’d find shelter before night. A car I could crawl into. A building with at least one room still intact. Something. Anything. As the hours slid by and the ruins refused to offer me even that, my steps grew slower. My thoughts fuzzed at the edges.

By the time the sky started bruising back toward evening, I was on autopilot. One foot, then the other. Breath in, breath out, every inhale burning, every exhale a white cloud. I was numb in places I knew I shouldn’t be numb. My right arm felt distant, almost separate from the rest of me. My ears rang faintly.

I scanned the buildings with what attention I had left. No intact doors. No obvious safe corners. Just more broken windows and collapsed roofs. “Really?” I croaked into the empty street. “I’m gonna die the first day I step outside?”

No answer, obviously.

 

My knees finally gave out. One moment I was upright. The next I was on the ground, cheek pressed against ice-cold concrete. I turned my head enough to keep my mouth out of the slush. The effort felt monumental. The world sounded distant, like I’d stuffed cotton in my ears. My own heartbeat thudded somewhere far away.

*So this is it,* I thought. *I leave the walls, and I die in the ruins, alone and stupid.* There was a certain sad symmetry to it. Livestock wandering outside the fence for the first time, only to collapse a few fields over.

I lay there for what could have been minutes or hours. Time stopped making sense. My thoughts unraveled one by one until only the simplest remained: cold, tired, hurts, sleep.

 

Somewhere inside that fog, a new sound slipped in. Soft. Quick. Not the heavy clank of boots or the mechanical whir of a drone. Lighter. Closer.

Sniffing.

Warm breath brushed my cheek. Something wet nudged my skin, then dragged across it in a rough, warm lick. My eyes fluttered open on instinct. The world swam into focus in jittery pieces. Fur. A black nose. Dark eyes studying me with sharp, animal curiosity. A dog.

I tried to move my hand. It didn’t quite cooperate. My chest rose once. Twice. The effort felt enormous. The edges of my vision darkened again.

I was so tired.

My eyes slipped closed without my permission.

And then there was nothing.

 

# Savior, Shelter and Survival

Warmth. That was the first thing my mind managed to process. Not the flat, mechanical heat of a safe-zone vent, but a softer, uneven warmth—fire, maybe. Something solid pressed against my back. A mattress. Thin, but a thousand times better than frozen concrete.

Light stabbed at my eyes when I forced them open. I blinked until the blur settled. I was in a small room with wooden walls—actual wood, darkened and warped with age. Faint cracks between the planks let in threads of cold air and the distant smell of snow. A small metal stove squatted in one corner, a fire crackling inside it, painting the room in unsteady orange.

No hum of generators. No flicker of LED strips. Just flame.

My right arm was wrapped in clean bandages, from wrist to shoulder. My chest, too. Someone had taken the time to patch me up. Someone had found me in the ruins and decided I was worth more than whatever bullets it took to put me down. That alone felt unreal.

On the small table beside the bed sat a glass of water and, next to it, an apple. A whole, actual apple. My stomach cramped at the sight.

 

I reached for the water first. My hand shook, but I managed not to spill as I brought it to my lips. The first swallow hurt—my throat was dry and sore—but the second felt like heaven. I drained the glass in seconds and set it down with a clumsy thunk.

Then I grabbed the apple. The skin broke under my teeth with a crisp snap. Juice flooded my mouth, cold and sweet and sharp. I devoured it in a handful of greedy bites, barely stopping to breathe. I hadn’t realized how hungry I was until that first taste hit my tongue.

When all that remained was the gnawed core, I dropped back against the pillow, staring up at the dark ceiling beams. The pain in my body had faded from blinding into a deep, persistent ache. My legs, though, felt strange—cramped and distant and wrong.

An ugly thought crept in. *What if I can’t move them?*

I swallowed, suddenly more afraid than I’d been at the wall. If I’d crippled myself my first day outside…

 

A bitter little laugh escaped me. “…might as well just end it,” I muttered. It came out more defeated than dramatic. The idea of being trapped in my own body, useless in a world built out of teeth, was worse than dying on that street.

Before the thought could really settle, I heard it: the slow, measured creak of footsteps on old wood. They drew closer, each one careful but heavy, heading straight for the door. My muscles tensed on reflex. Whoever this was had saved me. They’d also, technically, kidnapped me.

The handle turned. The door opened.

A man stepped in. He looked like someone the world had sanded down over a long time and hadn’t quite finished with. Middle-aged, maybe older, with a grey beanie pulled low over dark, messy hair and a beard that fell halfway down his chest. His clothes were rough and practical—thick fabric, faded from use, stained with dirt and smoke and work.

“You seem to be alright now,” he said.

That’s when I saw the pistol in his hand. It was pointed directly at me.

 

My mouth went dry.

“You a bandit,” he asked, his voice low and rough, “or just stupidly lost?”

“Lost,” I croaked automatically. “I’m just… lost.”

The barrel didn’t waver. His eyes, dark and steady, stayed on my face. “Lost how?” he pressed.

It took my brain a second to catch up. “I… I fled from the Berlin Safe Zone,” I said. The words felt strange, pulled into the open like a confession.

One of his eyebrows ticked up. “The Berlin Safe Zone?” he repeated. “You’re a long way from home. And ‘fled,’ huh?”

Most people out here would’ve traded a limb for a spot behind those walls. Saying I’d left on purpose sounded almost insane out loud.

 

“I wouldn’t necessarily say *fled*,” I backpedaled. “It was more of a… personal escape. The living conditions weren’t great. And it’s kind of been a dream of mine to see what’s out here.”

He made a sound that hovered somewhere between a grunt and a humorless chuckle. “A dream,” he said. “Dreams are fine. Not my place to kill a man for having one.”

The pistol lowered. He slid it into a worn holster at his side. My lungs remembered how to work.

“But you’d better brace yourself,” he added, looking me over like he was appraising old equipment. “If the living environment wasn’t good there, you’ve walked yourself straight into hell. Out here? It’s worse. A lot worse. And from the looks of you…” His gaze lingered on my bandaged arm, my too-thin frame. “…you won’t last a month.”

He wasn’t cruel about it. Just honest. Somehow, that stung more.

 

Shame and frustration twisted together in my chest. I dropped my gaze to the blanket, fingers curling weakly into the fabric. I’d almost died my first day outside. It was hard to argue with his assessment.

“Can you move?” he asked after a moment.

“Not really,” I admitted. “My legs feel numb. They’re cramped, though. Feels… wrong.”

Before he could answer, something slipped around his leg and into the room. A dog—medium-sized, with a shepherd’s build and an alert, clever face—trotted straight over to the bed. Its fur was a mix of browns and blacks, thick and rough. It sniffed at my hand, then my chest, tail giving a cautious wag.

The man sighed, a sound heavy with equal parts annoyance and fondness. “Figures,” he muttered.

Despite everything, I smiled. Carefully, I lifted my left hand and scratched behind the dog’s ear. It leaned into the touch immediately, tail wagging harder. The simple warmth of its body against mine felt almost unreal.

 

“What’s your name, boy?” the man asked.

“Alexander,” I said. “Alexander Colt.”

“Colt?” he repeated, something flickering behind his eyes for a heartbeat. Whatever memory it sparked, he let it go. “Name’s Karl Ottermand. That shepherd there is Viggo. He helps me scavenge what’s left.”

Viggo. The name suited him. He licked my fingers once, decisively.

“Nice to meet you, Karl,” I said, the words awkward on my tongue. “How long was I out?”

Karl glanced at the watch on his wrist. “’Bout eight hours,” he said.

Eight hours. I’d gone from almost dying in the snow to lying in a warm bed in less than half a day. My life had turned into one of the stories I used to read—only my arm hurt too much for it to feel fictional.

 

“You get some rest, boy,” Karl said, adjusting the strap of his holster. “Once you’re steady on your feet, I’ll teach you how to stay alive out here. Got it?”

The offer was so sudden I almost missed it. “Got it,” I replied.

He gave a short nod, whistled once. Viggo hopped off the bed and trotted to his side. Karl stepped out, holding the door open for the dog, then pulled it closed behind them.

I stared at the door for a long second, then sank back into the pillow. Saved by a stranger. Fed, patched up, offered survival lessons. Luck, I thought, might not hate me completely after all.

 

Morning announced itself as a thin bar of light slipping through a tear in the faded curtain. My body ached, but in a different way—less like I’d been smashed into asphalt and more like I’d slept too long in one position. Tentatively, I swung my legs over the side of the bed.

The wooden floor was cold under my feet. I held my breath and pushed myself upright. My legs wobbled, but they held. Relief hit so hard it made me dizzy. I wasn’t paralyzed. Just battered.

Slowly, using the wall for balance, I shuffled to the door and eased it open. The smell hit me before anything else.

Stew.

Rich, savory, heavy with herbs and something meaty. The scent wrapped around me like another blanket. My stomach made a noise I refused to acknowledge.

 

I followed it down a short hall into a larger room. Karl was crouched beside a small wood-burning stove, stirring a blackened pot. Steam billowed out as he lifted the lid, bringing the smell with it. Viggo lay curled up on a threadbare couch nearby, one ear perked lazily.

“You’re awake,” Karl said without looking up.

“Yeah,” I replied, hugging myself against the cold. “Isn’t it a little early for stew?”

“It’s winter,” he said flatly. “You’ve got a fever. And getting out of bed’s a stupid idea. I’ll bring it to you when it’s ready.”

There was no room for argument in his tone.

“Right. Uh… thanks,” I said, and retreated back to my room.

When I came in, something on the desk caught my eye. My notebook. And next to it, my favorite pen. Someone—Karl, obviously—had dug them out of my bag and set them within reach.

 

If I was really going to turn my life into a story, there was no better time to start.

I sat back on the bed, propped the notebook against my knees, and opened it to a blank page. For a moment, the emptiness stared back at me. Then the words started to come. The walls, the elevator, the truck, the fall, the dog. The fear and the ridiculousness and the fact that I was still somehow here. My handwriting was shaky, slanting harder than usual, but my hand didn’t stop moving.

About ten minutes later, the door creaked open again. Karl stepped in, balancing a wooden board across his hands. On it sat a steaming bowl of stew and a carved spoon. In his other hand, he held a glass of milk.

He set the board over my legs and placed the bowl carefully on top. The milk went on the desk beside the notebook. Steam curled up from the stew, carrying the smell of herbs and slow-cooked meat.

“Thank you,” I said, meaning it more than I expected.

“No problem, boy,” he replied. His eyes flicked to the notebook and pen. “What are you writing?”

“My dream,” I said.

 

He raised an eyebrow. I blew on a spoonful of stew to buy myself a heartbeat. It tasted even better than it smelled—earthy and rich, with chunks of mushrooms and something that was definitely meat and not something I wanted to think too hard about.

“I’ve always wanted to be part of a story,” I explained. “Reading’s been my way of… escaping. So I figured I’d write one I can actually live in.”

Karl’s expression softened by a degree. “So that’s what you meant by ‘personal escape,’ huh?” A low chuckle scraped out of him. “No shame in that.”

He nodded once, thoughtfully. “I’m guessing you’re writing a story you can stand being in.”

“Yahtzee,” I said, lifting the spoon in a small salute before taking another mouthful.

“This is amazing,” I added between bites, unable to hide my surprise. “What did you put in it?”

“Herbs. Bit of ground beef. Mushrooms Viggo sniffed out. Greens from the greenhouse,” he said, shrugging one shoulder. Then his tone shifted, steady and serious. “But let’s have a real talk.”

I felt my shoulders tense. “Okay…?”

 

“Once that fever’s gone,” he said, looking me straight in the eye, “we’re starting your guide to survival.”

The way he said it made it sound less like an offer and more like a sentence. I nodded anyway.

The fever lingered for a week. In that time, I learned things about Karl in pieces. He had once had a wife and a son. Both were gone now, taken in a mutant raid years back. He spoke about it like he’d worn the grief down to a smooth stone—accepted, but never really put down.

He also mentioned, almost in passing, that he’d been SED-4—Special Extermination Division. One of the elite. The kind of people safe zones relied on when something truly monstrous crawled out of the wasteland. Suddenly, his watchful stillness, his efficiency, the pistol always within reach, all of it made more sense.

What didn’t make sense was why he was still out here. A man like that could have gotten a comfortable slot inside a safe zone—training recruits, guarding a council, something. Instead, he lived in a half-buried barn with a dog and a stove. I didn’t ask why. Not yet.

 

When the fever finally broke and I stopped waking up drenched in sweat, Karl decided I was ready for “lesson one.”

He woke me before dawn. The world outside the window was still blue and half-formed. My body protested as I dragged myself out of bed and pulled on the thickest clothes I had. The cold slapped me in the face when I stepped outside.

The yard was a patch of trampled snow ringed by sagging fence posts and the leaning bulk of the barn. Karl stood near a battered wooden table, arms crossed, breath puffing in the air. A pistol sat on the table beside a rusted tin can. Viggo sat at his heel, ears forward, watching me.

“First thing you’re learning,” Karl said, “ain’t shooting. Ain’t hunting.” His gaze sharpened. “It’s trust.”

“Trust?” I echoed, teeth already starting to chatter. “You mean like… teamwork?”

He snorted. “Teamwork’s a pretty word for people who’ve never been betrayed.” He jerked his chin toward the table. “Pick it up.”

 

My eyes flicked to the pistol. “I don’t even—”

“Pick. It. Up.”

I did. The metal was bitterly cold in my hand. It felt heavier than it looked.

“Now point it at me,” he said.

I stared at him. “What?”

“You heard me.”

Every instinct screamed that this was a bad idea. My hand started to tremble before I even raised the gun. “Is this a joke?”

Karl didn’t move. “Lesson one, Alexander,” he said calmly. “Out here, trust’ll keep you alive longer than a bullet. But it can also get you killed faster. So tell me—do you trust me enough not to shoot you?”

 

Viggo whined quietly beside him, picking up the tension. My pulse thundered in my ears. “You’re the one who saved me,” I said. “Of course I trust you.”

“Then lower it,” he said.

I did, carefully angling the barrel toward the snow.

He nodded once. “Good. That’s your first mistake.”

Before I could blink, he moved. His hand snapped out, twisting my wrist just enough to make the gun fall. In the same motion, he caught it and pressed the barrel gently against my chest. His face didn’t change. He wasn’t angry. Just making a point.

“Never say you trust someone,” he said. “Make ’em earn it. Every damn day.”

He stepped back, flipped the gun in his hand, and offered it to me grip-first. “Lesson one complete.”

 

I took it, fingers stinging. My heart was still racing, but I understood. Out here, words were just air. Trust had to be proven, over and over, or it wasn’t worth betting your life on.

That night, after hours of drills that left my muscles shaking and my lungs burning, he sat me down by the stove and laid out the rest.

“For the next year,” Karl said, leaning forward in his chair, “you’re gonna learn three things.” He held up three thick fingers. “How to fight. How to hunt. And how to think. You can get by if you’re bad at one of ’em. Maybe two. Lose all three, and you’re already dead. Just waiting on something to notice.”

Viggo lay stretched out at his feet, tail thumping occasionally when Karl’s boot brushed his side. The fire snapped and spat in the stove, filling the silence between sentences.

 

“You’ll learn to track prey,” Karl continued, “and how to tell when *you’re* the prey. You’ll learn how to build traps, make fire without wasting fuel, skin something without ruining the meat. You’ll learn how to kill fast and clean when you have to. And you’ll learn to treat pain like rain—annoying, sometimes dangerous, but not a reason to stop moving.”

He turned his head, looking out the small window toward the white fields beyond. “Most important, you’ll learn to wait. Patience is what separates hunters from corpses. Out here, you’ll be both sooner or later. You just have to pick which one you are when it counts.”

He looked back at me. “You’re gonna hate me for a while,” he said. “You’ll curse me. Might even wish I’d left you in the snow. But if you’re still breathing after that year…” A faint, tired smirk tugged at his mouth. “…you’ll thank me.”

I didn’t believe him then. Not really. I nodded anyway.

 

The year that followed broke me and rebuilt me more times than I wanted to count.

Mornings started before dawn. Karl would shake me awake while the sky was still dark, shove a piece of bread into my hand, and have me out in the snow before my eyes were fully open. I learned to follow Viggo’s tracks through drifts and over ice, to recognize the difference between his gait and the prints of wild animals.

We ran strength drills until my lungs burned and my legs shook—hauling firewood, dragging sleds loaded with scrap, climbing the low hills beyond the barn over and over. When my arms screamed, he put a rifle in my hands and made me shoot until my aim stopped wobbling. When I failed, I repeated it. When I succeeded, I repeated it again until success felt boring.

He taught me how to read the land: how snow lay differently over buried debris; how broken branches or disturbed frost could mean something living had passed recently; which plants could be eaten and which would put me in the ground faster than any mutant.

 

I learned silence. I learned how to breathe so that the rise and fall of my chest didn’t shake the barrel of a gun. I learned how to lie still in the snow until numbness set in and wait for a target to make the first mistake.

And I learned, one small moment at a time, that trust really was something tested, not given.

Months bled into each other. The bruises changed, the scars accumulated, but somehow I kept waking up. Slowly, almost reluctantly, my body adapted. I stopped gasping halfway up the hill. My hands steadied. My shots landed closer to where I wanted them to.

Karl never once taught me how to fight another human. He never walked me through strategies for taking on mutants, either. “You’re not a soldier,” he would say whenever I pushed. “You’re a survivor. You only fight if running’s worse.”

When it came to open combat, I was still useless. But I could hunt. I could track. I could keep my hands moving even when my fingers felt like ice. I could breathe through fear without freezing.

 

One afternoon deep into winter, I moved through the skeletal remains of what had once been a small town, snow crunching under my boots. A rifle hung from my shoulder. My breath came out in steady white puffs. To my left, in the treeline, a guttural sound echoed—somewhere between a grunt and a roar. A moose.

I slipped behind a half-crumbling wall, raised the rifle, and waited. Two slow breaths. Sight, line, squeeze. The crack of the shot rolled across the ruins. Then silence.

Viggo padded ahead, nose to the ground, leading me to where the animal had fallen. He wagged his tail once, sharply, as if to say *good enough*.

Dragging the moose back to the barn was its own special hell. Karl had helped me rig a sled out of old sheet metal and rope, but “help” ended there. The animal’s weight turned each meter into a minor war. My shoulders screamed. My legs burned. My fingers ached inside my gloves.

 

But the barn’s roof eventually appeared over the rise, a familiar, lopsided silhouette against the gray sky. Smoke curled from the chimney. Home, for a certain battered definition of the word.

“Karl!” I shouted, or tried to, as I trudged into the yard. “I’m back with the moose!”

Viggo bounded ahead, circling the carcass with all the pride of a co-hunter. He sniffed, snorted, then trotted toward the barn, barking once.

Karl stepped out from around the corner, wiping his hands on a rag. For the first time since I’d met him, he was… smiling. Not the faint smirk he reserved for dark jokes. A real, if slightly rusty, smile.

“You’ve caught yourself a big one, Colt,” he said, clapping a hand on my back hard enough to jolt my teeth. “Get ’im hung up so we can skin it. I’ll prepare a moose roast.”

A roast. Not stew. The word alone felt like a celebration.

 

As I hauled the carcass toward the hooks in the barn, a knot of nervous curiosity twisted in my stomach. Karl had been acting a little different lately—quieter in a softer way, watching me with an expression I couldn’t quite parse.

It unsettled me. But I didn’t mind the idea of a special meal.

By the time evening rolled around, the smell drifting through the house was enough to make me dizzy. Karl had gone all out. I sat at the rough wooden table, fingers tapping restlessly against the surface, listening to the small clatter of dishes in the next room.

I was nervous. I didn’t know why. It felt like waiting for an exam you hadn’t studied for.

 

Karl came in carrying two plates. He set one in front of me and one at his place across the table. Thick slices of roasted moose lay under a glossy brown sauce, flanked by potatoes, carrots, and actual greens. Viggo sat hopefully at Karl’s side, eyes locked on the meat.

Karl tore off a piece and flicked it to the dog. “There you go, buddy,” he murmured, then looked up at me.

“What’s with all this?” I asked, trying—and failing—to sound casual. “Feels a little… extra, don’t you think?”

He let out a slow breath through his nose, like he’d been expecting the question.

“Colt,” he said, voice softer than I’d ever heard it, “this past year, you’ve filled a gap in my heart.” He held my gaze, the corner of his mouth lifting in that rare, real smile. “To me… you’re starting to look like my son.”

The words hit harder than any training blow.

 

For a moment, I forgot how to breathe. My throat tightened. My fingers clenched around the edge of the table.

“And I want to thank you for that,” he continued. “For reminding me that joy can still be found, even in a world like this.”

He straightened slightly, shoulders squaring the way they did when he was about to say something important. “Today marks one year since I pulled you out of the snow,” he said. “So tonight, I wanted to make it special. For you.”

The room was quiet except for the faint crackle of the fire and Viggo’s tail thumping softly against the floor. The plates in front of us steamed gently, a ridiculous luxury in a dead world.

I opened my mouth, searching for something clever, something grateful, something that matched the weight of what he’d just given me. All that came out was a hoarse, honest:

“Thank you.”

It didn’t feel like enough. But the look in Karl’s eyes said it was a start.

I picked up my fork, took a bite, and decided that if this was a story I was writing, this would be the end of the first chapter of my real life: not the moment I left the walls, but the moment I found a reason to keep going once I did.
